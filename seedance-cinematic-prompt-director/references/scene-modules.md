# Seedance Scene Modules

Use this when the prompt belongs to a specific scene type.

## Action or Fight

Prioritize physical clarity over speed.

Include:

- Who initiates and from where.
- Specific attack path or movement.
- Defense, failure, or impact.
- Reaction and recovery.
- Screen direction.
- What the camera tracks.

Avoid endless combos, teleporting bodies, impact without reaction, and
decoration: not "epic battle" — describe the physical path of each strike and
its consequence.

## Chase

Include:

- Pursuer and pursued.
- Terrain or obstacles.
- Distance changing over time.
- Camera relation to bodies.
- Breath, footfalls, impacts, and environmental sound.

Avoid: not "thrilling chase" or "heart-pounding" — describe the distance
closing, the breathing getting ragged, the obstacle that almost stops them.

## Horror or Suspense

Include:

- What is seen, what is hidden, and when the reveal happens.
- Sound focus: silence, room tone, breath, distant movement.
- Camera restriction: locked, slow push, handheld, blocked view.
- Character behavior before fear becomes obvious.

Avoid: not "terrifying" or "creepy atmosphere" — describe what is hidden, what
sound breaks the silence, and what the character's body does before fear shows
on their face.

## Romance or Intimacy

Include:

- Micro-behavior: hesitation, eye contact, breath, hand movement.
- Spatial distance and how it changes.
- What remains unsaid.
- Soft or restrained camera movement.

Avoid: not "passionate" or "beautiful moment" — describe the exact distance
between their hands, the breath held half a second too long, the eye contact
that breaks first.

## Dialogue

Include:

- One visible emotional turn.
- Actor behavior while listening.
- Blocking and eyeline.
- Subtext in performance, not long dialogue.

Avoid: not "powerful dialogue" or "emotional conversation" — show the single
moment where the listener's face changes. That is the scene.

## Product or Fashion

Include:

- Product identity and surface details.
- Motion that reveals texture, scale, or use.
- Light behavior on material.
- Clean background logic.

Avoid: not "elegant" or "premium quality" — describe the material surface, the
way light bends on the edge, the motion that reveals scale.

## Commercial & Product Promo

Use this for advertising clips, brand videos, product launches, and promotional
content.

Structure:

```text
Duration:        (recommended 6-10 seconds per segment)
Product/Brand:   (name, category, key visual identity)
Hero Moment:     (the single most compelling visual — the money shot)
Motion:          (how the product enters, reveals, or transforms)
Surface Detail:  (texture, material, reflection, color accuracy)
Environment:     (studio, lifestyle setting, abstract — describe fully)
Lighting:        (product lighting is critical — specify key, fill, rim, and background)
Camera:          (slow orbit, push in, macro detail, reveal pull-back)
Text/Logo:       (if on-screen text needed, describe placement and timing)
Sound:           (product sound, ambient, music bed — specify energy and style)
CTA:             (call-to-action moment — what the viewer should feel/do at the end)
```

### Commercial Beat Patterns

| Pattern        | Structure                                          | Best For                 |
| -------------- | -------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------ |
| Reveal         | Dark/empty → product enters → hero light → hold    | Luxury, tech, premium    |
| Lifestyle      | Person using product → reaction → product close-up | Food, cosmetics, fitness |
| Transformation | Before state → product interaction → after state   | Cleaning, beauty, tools  |
| Montage        | Multiple quick angles of product → hero shot       | Fashion, accessories     |

### Commercial Anti-Slop

These words are especially common and especially useless in commercial prompts:

- "premium quality" → describe the material and light behavior on the surface.
- "innovative design" → show the design feature in motion.
- "luxury feel" → specify: slow camera, dark background, rim light on edges,
  matte surface.

## Music Video — Rhythm Sync

Use this for performance clips, dance sequences, and audio-synced visuals.

Include:

- Performance energy and intensity level.
- Rhythm of cuts or movement — sync to audio beat if audio reference is
  provided.
- Color and light motif that repeats across shots.
- Repeated gesture, prop, or visual hook.

### Rhythm Sync Structure

```text
Duration:
Audio Description:   (track name, genre, instruments, mood)
BPM/Energy:          (slow/medium/fast — or specific BPM if known)
Visual Pace:         (slow/medium/fast editing cuts, camera movement speed)
Performance:         (lip sync? dance? instrument? physical performance?)
Visual Motif:        (recurring color, prop, gesture, or composition)
Camera Sync:         (does the camera move with the rhythm? static during slow parts, moving during fast parts?)
Lighting Sync:       (does lighting change with energy? e.g., warm verse → cool chorus?)
```

### Music Video Shot Rhythm

- Verse: tighter shots, steadier camera, lower energy movement.
- Pre-chorus: camera begins to move, energy builds, color shifts.
- Chorus: widest or most dynamic shot, peak movement, strongest light.
- Bridge: contrast — go quiet, go tight, go dark. Break the pattern.

## Educational & Explainer

Use this for instructional content, how-to demonstrations, tutorials, product
walkthroughs, and informational videos.

Structure:

```text
Duration:            (recommended 8-12 seconds per segment)
Subject:             (what is being taught or explained)
Visual Approach:     (live demonstration, animated diagram, screen capture, hybrid)
Step Shown:          (one clear step per clip — not the entire process)
Key Visual:          (what the viewer must see clearly to understand the step)
Clarity Priority:    (what must stay in focus and unobstructed)
Camera:              (usually static or slow push — prioritize clarity over style)
Annotation Zone:     (where on-screen text or labels would appear — describe reserved space)
Lighting:            (even, well-lit — shadows should not obscure the subject)
Sound:               (narration-ready silence, or ambient appropriate to setting)
Pacing:              (slow enough for comprehension — no fast cuts)
```

### Educational Principles

1. **One concept per clip.** If you need to show three steps, make three clips.
2. **Clarity over beauty.** Even lighting, clear framing, no distracting
   backgrounds. If the viewer cannot see the key visual clearly, the clip fails.
3. **Leave space for text.** Educational content often needs on-screen labels,
   arrows, or captions. Describe a "clean zone" in the frame where text can be
   overlaid.
4. **Consistent framing across a series.** If this is one step in a sequence,
   maintain the same camera angle, distance, and lighting for all steps.
5. **Show the result.** End each clip with a brief hold on the completed state
   so the viewer can confirm understanding.

### Educational Anti-Slop

- "easy to understand" → (delete — clarity comes from framing, not adjectives)
- "professional quality" → (delete — describe the actual setup)
- "engaging and dynamic" → specify: what moves, what stays still, where the eye
  goes
