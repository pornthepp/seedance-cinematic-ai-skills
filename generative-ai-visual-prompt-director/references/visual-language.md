# Visual Language

Use this guide to choose precise terms for image and video prompts. Select only
the details that support the brief; specificity is useful, accumulation is not.

## Subject

Define the subject through distinguishing visible traits rather than a generic
label.

Include, when relevant:

- **Identity:** person, creature, object, product, vehicle, or place.
- **Count and relationship:** one child, two rivals, a clustered crowd.
- **Physical anchors:** silhouette, age range, build, hair, markings, geometry.
- **Wardrobe and props:** materials, fit, condition, culturally accurate details.
- **State:** wet, dusty, damaged, polished, exhausted, alert.

Use three to five stable anchors for recurring characters or products. Keep the
same anchor wording across shots. Avoid adding traits that cannot be visible in
the requested framing.

## Action and Performance

Use filmable verbs and describe their visible phase.

| Intent | More visible wording |
| --- | --- |
| walking | strides left to right, coat trailing behind |
| thinking | pauses over an unopened letter, eyes fixed downward |
| afraid | shoulders raised, weight shifting backward, shallow breath |
| celebrating | jumps with both arms raised as confetti falls |
| reaching | fingertips extend toward the handle, not yet touching it |

For an image, choose the most readable instant: anticipation, contact, peak
action, or aftermath. For video, define direction, pace, rhythm, and the change
from the starting state to the ending state.

## Environment

An environment should locate the subject and contribute to the story.

Specify:

- place and scale;
- time of day, season, weather, and period;
- two or three meaningful landmarks or objects;
- atmosphere such as mist, dust, rain, smoke, heat shimmer, or clear air;
- spatial relationship: subject at doorway, behind glass, beneath structure;
- foreground, midground, and background when depth matters.

Avoid inventories of unrelated props. Choose details that reveal use, history,
status, danger, or emotional contrast.

## Composition

### Shot Size

| Choice | Best for | Risk |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Extreme close-up | texture, eyes, symbolic detail | loses context |
| Close-up | emotion and recognition | weak body action |
| Medium shot | gesture, conversation, relationship | less environmental scale |
| Full shot | complete body action and wardrobe | weaker facial nuance |
| Wide shot | subject within a place | subject may become secondary |
| Extreme wide shot | scale, isolation, geography | identity detail may disappear |

### Placement and Balance

- **Centered symmetry:** order, authority, ritual, confrontation.
- **Off-center balance:** naturalism, tension, implied movement.
- **Negative space:** isolation, anticipation, room for text or gaze.
- **Foreground framing:** intimacy, surveillance, obstruction, depth.
- **Layered depth:** foreground, subject plane, and background for dimensionality.
- **Diagonals:** energy, instability, acceleration.
- **Horizontal structure:** calm, distance, rest.
- **Vertical structure:** height, aspiration, confinement, power.

Respect gaze room and lead room unless deliberate compression should make the
subject feel trapped. State where the subject sits in the frame when placement
is important.

## Camera and Viewpoint

Separate shot size, subject direction, camera height, camera angle, and lens
feeling. They solve different problems.

### Subject Direction

- **Front view:** direct, open, confrontational.
- **Profile:** graphic silhouette, observation, forward intent.
- **Three-quarter view:** dimensional face and natural portrait balance.
- **Back view:** anonymity, departure, discovery, shared observation.
- **Over-the-shoulder:** conversation, pursuit, subjective access.
- **Point of view:** immediate identification with a character.

### Height and Angle

| Choice | Typical effect |
| --- | --- |
| Eye level | neutral, human, conversational |
| Low angle | scale, dominance, threat, wonder |
| High angle | vulnerability, overview, reduced power |
| Ground level | tactile foreground, speed, small-creature viewpoint |
| Top-down | pattern, organization, detachment, geography |
| Bird's-eye | large-scale relationships and isolation |
| Dutch angle | instability or disorientation; use with motivation |

Angle alone does not create emotion. Subject posture, composition, and light
must support the same reading.

### Lens Feeling

- **Wide feeling:** expanded space, stronger perspective, energetic proximity;
  may distort subjects near frame edges.
- **Normal feeling:** balanced perspective, observational realism.
- **Telephoto feeling:** compressed depth, isolation, graphic layering,
  surveillance or intimacy from distance.
- **Macro feeling:** minute surface detail and a very narrow focus plane.

Use focal length numbers only when technically useful. Emotional and spatial
behavior is more portable across generation systems.

## Lighting

Describe a coherent lighting system:

1. **Source:** sun, window, overcast sky, lamp, signage, fire, screen.
2. **Direction:** front, side, back, top, underlight, or wraparound.
3. **Quality:** hard, soft, diffused, dappled, hazy, specular.
4. **Contrast:** low-key, high-key, deep shadow, open shadow, silhouette.
5. **Color relationship:** warm source with cool ambient, neutral daylight,
   colored practicals.
6. **Effect:** separation, concealment, tenderness, exposure, danger.

Motivated light has a believable source within or just outside the scene. Use
rim light for separation, side light for form and texture, frontal soft light
for clarity, and backlight for silhouette or atmosphere. Do not stack several
dramatic sources without explaining their relationship.

## Color

Build a palette as relationships, not a list of color names.

- **Dominant color:** controls the overall world.
- **Supporting colors:** shape depth and material separation.
- **Accent color:** directs attention to one important element.
- **Temperature:** warm, cool, or a motivated warm-cool contrast.
- **Saturation:** restrained, selective, muted, or vivid.
- **Value structure:** bright subject on dark ground, dark silhouette on bright
  ground, or compressed low contrast.

Color meaning depends on culture and context. Tie palette choices to the scene's
story function. Preserve believable skin, product, or brand colors when they are
continuity anchors.

## Material and Texture

Name what the surface is made of and how it interacts with light.

| Material quality | Useful cues |
| --- | --- |
| polished metal | crisp reflections, cool highlights, fine scratches |
| brushed metal | directional grain, broad soft reflections |
| aged wood | open grain, worn edges, uneven stain |
| translucent fabric | layered folds, transmitted backlight, soft edges |
| weathered skin | pores, fine lines, uneven sheen, natural variation |
| wet pavement | darkened surface, broken reflections, small ripples |
| matte ceramic | diffuse highlight, fine texture, restrained reflection |

Avoid the generic word `detailed` when a surface behavior can be named. Add
wear, imperfections, residue, stitching, grain, condensation, or patina only
where they support scale and history.

## Mood and Style

Mood should emerge from a coordinated set of visible choices.

```text
mood = composition + performance + light + color + atmosphere + rhythm
```

Examples:

- **Hopeful:** open horizon, upward posture, warm edge light, clear air.
- **Uneasy:** off-center framing, blocked sightlines, mixed color temperature,
  slight stillness before motion.
- **Nostalgic:** gentle contrast, softened highlight rolloff, worn materials,
  familiar domestic detail.
- **Clinical:** precise geometry, flat cool light, clean surfaces, minimal color.
- **Mythic:** strong silhouette, monumental scale, elemental atmosphere,
  deliberate symmetry.

Describe style through medium, rendering, shape language, edge quality, texture,
contrast, and era. Prefer `flat geometric shapes, limited screen-print palette,
rough ink edges` over naming a living artist.

## Motion for Video

Treat motion as three coordinated layers.

### Subject Motion

State the action, direction, speed, weight, and performance. Use restrained
micro-movement for intimacy; use clear whole-body movement for readable action.

### Camera Motion

- **Locked:** observation, tension, graphic control.
- **Pan or tilt:** reveals information across space.
- **Dolly in/out:** changes emotional distance.
- **Tracking:** travels with the subject and preserves relationship.
- **Orbit:** reveals form or shifts power relationships.
- **Handheld drift:** embodied uncertainty; keep amplitude intentional.
- **Crane or aerial move:** reveals scale and geography.

Name a movement only when it has a story purpose. Define its pace and endpoint.

### Environment Motion

Use wind, fabric, hair, rain, dust, steam, water, foliage, crowds, reflections,
or moving light to make the space feel alive. Keep this motion subordinate to
the subject unless the environment is the event.

### Continuity

Across a clip or sequence, hold stable:

- subject identity, proportions, wardrobe, and props;
- screen direction, eyeline, and scene geography;
- time of day, key-light direction, and palette;
- material condition and environmental state;
- the exact state reached at the end of the previous shot.

Use stillness deliberately. A locked camera and a nearly motionless subject can
create more tension than constant movement.
