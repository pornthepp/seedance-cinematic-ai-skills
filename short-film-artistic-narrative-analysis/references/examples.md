# Short Film Analysis Examples

Worked examples showing input and full output.

## Example 1: Script Critique With Rewrite Priorities

### User Input

> Here's my short film concept: A janitor at a museum stays after closing. He talks to the paintings. One painting talks back. They have a conversation about loneliness. He goes home feeling better. 6 minutes. What's working and what isn't?

### Output

**Core dramatic engine:** A lonely man finds connection in an impossible place. The question is: does the painting's response come from magic, madness, or his own need? The film's power depends on which answer the audience takes home.

**What is working:**

- The setting is strong. A museum after closing is visually cinematic — empty galleries, security lights, large paintings in shadow. Production design is built in.
- The janitor-as-protagonist works because his relationship to the art is physical (he cleans around it every night) but never intellectual (he is not a curator or critic). This creates a character who is intimate with the paintings but sees them differently.
- The single-location structure suits 6 minutes.

**Main risk:**

The conversation about loneliness is the weak point. Two characters talking about being lonely is a short film's most common failure mode — it collapses a visual medium into a radio play. The painting cannot move, so it is just a voice. The janitor standing still and talking is not cinema.

**Structural notes:**

| Priority | Issue | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Intensify** | The conversation is too safe. He talks, the painting talks. Nobody is changed, challenged, or hurt. | The painting should say something the janitor does not want to hear — something true about why he is alone. The conversation should have a cost. |
| **Cut** | "He goes home feeling better" is a resolved ending that defuses the emotional charge. | End in the museum. Let the last moment be ambiguous — is he comforted or more alone than before? The audience should carry the question home. |
| **Clarify** | It is unclear whether the painting is actually talking. If it is magic, the film becomes fantasy. If it is imagination, it becomes psychological. | Choose one and commit — or make the ambiguity the point. If ambiguous, show the janitor's uncertainty: does he believe what he's hearing? A moment of doubt mid-conversation sharpens everything. |
| **Intensify** | The janitor "talks to the paintings" is too passive. What prompts tonight to be different? | Add a catalyst. Tonight is not routine. Something happened today — he was ignored, dismissed, insulted, or received bad news. Tonight he needs the paintings to talk back. |
| **Keep** | The setting and the character's physical relationship to art. | Protect the cleaning-as-ritual opening. Show him mopping around a masterpiece without looking at it. That image says more about his loneliness than any dialogue. |

**Character notes:**

- The janitor needs a specific wound, not generic loneliness. "Lonely" is a condition. "His wife died two years ago and he has not said her name out loud since" is a story. Give the loneliness a shape.
- The painting should have a personality that is not simply "wise and kind." If the painting is a portrait, who was the subject? What would that person actually say to a janitor who talks to walls? A 17th-century merchant might be rude. A saint might be silent. A portrait of a young woman might ask him questions instead of giving answers.

**Visual/sound notes:**

- The museum after hours has a sound identity: echo, HVAC hum, the squeak of his mop bucket wheels, a distant security alarm beep. Build this soundscape before any voice appears. When the painting speaks, the ambient sound should change — drop slightly, or shift — so the audience feels the threshold crossing.
- Lighting: security lights create pools of illumination with darkness between them. The janitor moves between light and dark. The painting that speaks should be in a shaft of light that looks different from the security lights — warmer, as if it generates its own atmosphere.
- The camera should be close to the janitor when he cleans (this is his world, physical and grounded) and wider when he stands before the painting (he is small in front of the art — reversed power dynamic).

**Concrete rewrite actions:**

1. Add a catalyst: what happened today that makes tonight different?
2. Give the janitor a specific loss, not generic loneliness.
3. Give the painting a personality that challenges the janitor, not just comforts him.
4. Replace part of the dialogue with physical action — what does the janitor DO while talking? Does he keep cleaning? Does he sit down? Does he touch the frame?
5. Cut the "goes home" ending. End on his face in the museum — the audience decides whether he found something real or is deeper in his isolation.

## Example 2: Festival-Facing Feedback

### User Input

> My 12-minute short is about a single mother who steals food from the hotel where she works as a cleaner so her kids can eat. She gets caught. She loses her job. The end. I want to submit to festivals. What's the festival jury going to think?

### Output

**Core dramatic engine:** Survival under a system designed to punish survival. The moral question is real: is stealing food wrong when your children are hungry and your employer pays you less than food costs?

**What a festival jury will recognize:**

- Social realism with a strong moral dilemma — this genre has a proven festival track record (Dardenne brothers, Ken Loach, Hirokazu Kore-eda).
- The class tension between "cleaner" and "hotel" is instantly visual — she handles luxury she cannot afford.
- 12 minutes is a good length for this story.

**What a festival jury will question:**

**1. Suffering without agency.**

She steals. She gets caught. She loses her job. In this structure, things happen TO her. She never makes a choice that costs her something. Stealing food when your kids are hungry is not a moral choice — it is necessity. The story needs a moment where she faces a real dilemma with no clean answer.

**Upgrade:** What if she could avoid getting caught — but only by doing something that betrays someone else? Another cleaner who covered for her, a guest who left the door open. The moral complexity arrives when survival requires hurting someone who helped you.

**2. Poverty as the only characterization.**

Festival juries see hundreds of films about poor people suffering. What they rarely see is a poor character who is funny, vain, stubborn, ambitious, petty, or brilliant. If the mother is defined entirely by her poverty and her maternal sacrifice, she becomes a symbol, not a person.

**Upgrade:** Give her one trait that has nothing to do with poverty. She is an excellent liar. She is obsessed with keeping her nails perfect. She memorizes the hotel guests' conversations and retells them to her kids as bedtime stories. Something that makes her specific.

**3. The ending is punishment, not cinema.**

"She loses her job" is a plot event, not an ending. It tells us what happens but not what it means. A festival short should end on an image, a silence, or a moment that keeps expanding after the screen goes black.

**Upgrade options:**

- She loses the job. The manager hands her a garbage bag with her locker contents. She walks out the service entrance. The camera holds on the service door for 5 seconds after she leaves. Then a new cleaner comes through with a cart. The system replaces her instantly. That image is the ending.
- She is fired. She walks home. She enters the apartment with a bag of stolen food she had already hidden before getting caught. She serves it to her children. She watches them eat. She says nothing. The audience knows this is the last stolen meal.
- She is caught but not by management — by the other cleaner who has been covering for her. The other cleaner has to choose: report or stay silent and risk her own job. End on the other cleaner's face. The moral weight transfers to a new character.

**Structural note:** 12 minutes gives you room for 2-3 stealing sequences before the catch. Use them to show escalation: the first theft is small (a bread roll), the second is bolder (a full tray from room service), the third is the one that gets her caught. Each theft should feel different — different risk, different method, different emotional state. The escalation IS the story.

**Visual note:** The hotel is full of food she cannot eat. Every frame should remind us: buffet tables, room service carts, guests leaving half-eaten plates. She cleans around abundance. This contrast does more work than any dialogue about poverty.

## Example 3: Diagnosing Why A Short Film Feels Long

### User Input

> My 7-minute short film about a couple breaking up in a car feels like it drags. I've tried cutting but it still feels slow. What's wrong?

### Output

**Common reasons a car-breakup scene drags (checking each):**

**1. The conversation goes in circles.**

If both characters repeat their positions without escalation, the audience checks out after the first cycle. "I can't do this anymore" / "Why?" / "You know why" / "No I don't" / "I can't do this anymore" — this is a loop, not a scene.

**Fix:** Map the conversation as a staircase, not a circle. Each exchange must reach a new level — a new accusation, a new confession, a new piece of information that changes what the audience knows. If any 30-second stretch could be moved to a different point in the scene without the audience noticing, that stretch is a loop. Cut it.

**2. No physical action.**

Two people sitting in a car talking is visually static. The audience's eye has nowhere to go. The image does not change, so the brain stops processing visual information and the scene feels like audio-only.

**Fix:** Use the car. Hands on the steering wheel tightening. Eyes checking the mirror. A hand reaching for the door handle and pulling back. The engine is on or off? Does someone turn the radio on, then off? Does one character start the car mid-argument as a power move? Does the windshield fog up from their breathing? Physical actions give the audience something to watch between lines.

**3. The emotional level is flat.**

If both characters are at the same level of anger/sadness from start to finish, there is no shape. A 7-minute scene at intensity level 7 feels longer than a scene that goes 3→8→2→9.

**Fix:** Start lower than you think. Let the first minute feel almost normal — maybe even light. The audience should not immediately know this is a breakup scene. Then let it escalate through specific triggers, not general frustration. Drop the energy after a particularly harsh line — a silence where someone almost takes it back. Then escalate again higher than before. The variation creates rhythm, and rhythm creates the feeling of movement.

**4. The ending is reached too early but the scene continues.**

Sometimes the breakup decision is effectively made at minute 4, and the remaining 3 minutes are aftermath that has no new information. The audience already knows the outcome and is waiting for the characters to catch up.

**Fix:** Identify the point of no return — the exact line or action after which reconciliation is impossible. Everything before it is the scene. Everything after it should be 30 seconds maximum — a silence, a door opening, an engine starting. If your point of no return is at minute 4, you have a 4.5-minute short, not a 7-minute one.

**5. The car is never used as a space with meaning.**

A parked car is a trap — neither person can leave without making a dramatic decision (opening the door, stepping out, walking away). An idling car adds a layer — are we going or staying? A moving car adds another — one person controls the vehicle while the other cannot leave at all. Where the car is parked matters — in their driveway (the home they're losing), at a gas station (nowhere, transient), on the side of a highway (dangerous, exposed).

**Fix:** Decide where the car is and why. Use the location. If they are in their own driveway, the house they shared is visible through the windshield the entire time — a visual reminder of what is being lost. If they are at a red light, the light turns green, someone honks, and they have to either drive or get out. The external world applies pressure.

**Diagnosis priority:** If you have already tried cutting dialogue and it still drags, the problem is likely #3 (flat emotional shape) or #4 (the scene ends before the scene ends). Try mapping the emotional level of each minute on a 1-10 scale. If the graph is a flat line, that is your answer.
