# Acting Performance Examples

Worked examples showing input and full output.

## Example 1: Turning Emotion Into Playable Direction

### User Input

> My character is a doctor who just lost a patient. She has to go tell the family. She's devastated. How do I direct this?

### Output

**Problem with "she's devastated":** It is a result, not a direction. An actor cannot play "devastated" — they can only play actions that produce devastation in the audience. Directing emotion produces indicating (showing the feeling instead of living through the behavior).

**Given circumstances:**

- Who: Dr. Lena, mid-30s, surgical resident, 14 hours into her shift.
- Where: Hospital corridor between the OR and the waiting room. Fluorescent lights, linoleum floor, other staff walking past.
- When: 2 minutes after calling time of death.
- Relationship: She met the family for 4 minutes before surgery. She does not know them well. The patient was a 10-year-old boy.
- Pressure: Other surgeries are waiting. She cannot take time. The attending is watching how she handles it.

**Objective:** Get through the next 90 seconds without breaking in front of the family.

**Obstacle:** Her body is betraying her. Her hands are shaking. Her throat is tightening. She has to be professional in front of people who are about to hear the worst news of their lives.

**Tactics by beat:**

| Beat | What Happens | Tactic (Playable Verb) | Physical Behavior |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 — Walking | She walks from the OR to the waiting room | **Control.** She is organizing herself. | She takes off surgical cap, folds it precisely, puts it in her coat pocket. Smooths her coat. Slows her pace. These are rituals of composure. Her hands are busy so they cannot shake. |
| 2 — Door | She reaches the waiting room door | **Prepare.** She rehearses the words. | She stops before the door. Touches the door handle but does not push it yet. Takes one breath. Her lips move slightly — she is mouthing the opening line to herself. Jaw tightens. She pushes the door. |
| 3 — Delivery | She tells the family | **Protect.** She is trying to protect them AND herself from the full weight. | She makes eye contact with the mother. She speaks in a measured, steady voice — too steady. The words are clinical: "We did everything we could. He did not survive the surgery." Her hands are clasped in front of her, still. She does not touch the family. She is holding a professional distance that is also a dam. |
| 4 — Break | The mother's reaction cracks her composure | **Contain.** She is trying not to cry. | The mother makes a sound. Dr. Lena's jaw muscle contracts. She looks down for half a second — the only visible crack. She breathes in through her nose. Her clasped hands tighten. She looks up again. Her eyes are wet but she does not blink. She will not cry here. |

**Voice/delivery note:** Her speech in Beat 3 should feel rehearsed — slightly too smooth, with pauses in odd places, as if she is following a script she wrote in her head during the walk. This is the sound of someone performing composure. When the mother reacts in Beat 4, Lena's next words should come slightly late — a half-second gap where the mask almost slips.

**Subtext:** "I am not allowed to fall apart. I am not allowed to fall apart. I am not allowed to fall apart."

**Direction note for the actor:**

```text
In this scene, you want to get through the notification without losing control. Your obstacle is your own grief — it is physical, in your throat and hands. Try to contain it by keeping your hands busy, your voice steady, and your posture upright. Do not show sadness directly. Let it leak through the things you cannot control: the half-second delay before speaking, the jaw tightening, the hands clasping harder. The audience should see someone trying very hard not to feel, and failing at the edges.
```

## Example 2: Rewriting A Generic Performance Prompt For AI Video

### User Input

> My video prompt says the character should "look scared and run away in terror." Can you make the performance better?

### Output

**Problem:** "Look scared and run away in terror" produces generic fear — wide eyes, open mouth, running. It has no specificity, no character, no physical detail that would make this one person's fear instead of a stock reaction.

**Questions that shape the fix:**

1. What is the character afraid of? (A person? An animal? A sound? Something they saw?)
2. How far away is the threat?
3. Does the character expect the threat or is it a surprise?
4. What was the character doing right before the fear?

**Three different versions of "scared and running" based on different answers:**

**Version A — Sudden surprise, close threat:**

```text
The woman freezes mid-step. Her whole body locks — shoulders rise, arms pull in tight against her torso, chin drops. A sharp inhale, mouth closed. Her eyes fix on a point directly ahead. For one second she does not move. Then she pivots on her back foot and runs — not a cinematic sprint, but an ugly, uncoordinated scramble. She stumbles on the first step, catches herself with one hand on the ground, pushes off, and accelerates with her body leaning too far forward, arms pumping unevenly.
```

**Version B — Growing dread, distant threat approaching:**

```text
The man takes a step backward. Then another. His retreat is slow — he is not running yet. His breathing quickens. He glances behind him to check the escape path, then looks back at the approaching threat. His hands rise to chest level, palms open — a primal "stop" gesture that he is not aware of making. His jaw clenches. When he finally turns to run, the first three steps are controlled, deliberate — then he breaks into a flat sprint, looking over his shoulder once, and does not look back again.
```

**Version C — Paralyzed fear, cannot run:**

```text
The woman sees it and does not move. Her knees bend slightly, lowering her center of gravity — an animal instinct. Her right hand reaches out and finds the wall beside her. She grips it. Her breathing is shallow, rapid, audible. Her eyes are locked open but she is not scanning — she is fixed on one point. Her left hand trembles. She wants to run but her body has chosen freeze instead of flight. Her lips part. No sound comes out.
```

**The difference:** All three are "scared." But Version A is surprise-fear (fight-or-flight with no processing time), Version B is anticipatory fear (the mind is working, calculating escape), and Version C is paralysis fear (the body overrides the mind). Each produces a completely different physical performance. "Look scared" collapses all of these into one flat instruction.

## Example 3: Beat Breakdown For A Dialogue Scene

### User Input

> Two former best friends meet at a funeral. They haven't spoken in 3 years. One of them (Maya) wants to reconnect. The other (Sara) does not. They have a short conversation. I need acting beats.

### Output

**Given circumstances:**

- Who: Maya (initiator, wants reconciliation) and Sara (resistor, wants to maintain distance)
- Where: Outside a church after a funeral. Other mourners around but not close. Both holding paper cups of coffee.
- When: After the service, before people leave.
- Relationship: Former best friends since college. The fallout was over something Sara did that hurt Maya. Maya has processed it. Sara has not processed being the one who was wrong.
- Pressure: Public setting — neither can make a scene. The funeral creates emotional vulnerability that lowers defenses.

**Maya's objective:** Reach Sara. Prove that the friendship is worth more than the wound.

**Sara's objective:** Get through this encounter without reopening what she did. Self-protection disguised as indifference.

| Beat | Trigger | Maya's Tactic | Sara's Tactic | Maya's Body | Sara's Body |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 — Approach | Maya sees Sara standing alone and walks toward her | **Test.** She approaches casually, as if this is not a big deal. | **Brace.** She sees Maya coming and prepares a surface. | Maya walks at a normal pace. She does not rush. She holds her coffee with both hands. Small smile — warm but uncertain. | Sara sees Maya, shifts her weight to her back foot. Does not walk away but does not open her body. Takes a sip of coffee — the cup becomes a prop/shield. |
| 2 — Opening | Maya says something neutral about the deceased | **Disarm.** She starts with safe territory. No accusations, no history. | **Deflect.** She responds politely but briefly. Keep it transactional. | Maya stands at a respectful distance — about 4 feet. She speaks at a normal volume. Her tone is gentle, not careful. | Sara nods. Gives a short answer. Makes brief eye contact then looks at the ground. She is counting the seconds until she can politely leave. |
| 3 — Reach | Maya says something that references their shared past — a memory of the three of them (the deceased, Maya, Sara) | **Invite.** She is offering Sara a bridge back. | **Resist.** The memory threatens her wall. She cannot engage with it. | Maya's voice softens. She lets herself be vulnerable — she is not pretending this is easy. She looks directly at Sara. | Sara's jaw tightens. She swallows. She looks away at the parking lot. Her free hand finds her coat pocket. She is physically closing. |
| 4 — Break | Sara says she has to go. Maya says her name — just the name, nothing else. | **Hold.** She will not chase, but she will not let it end as if it did not matter. | **Escape.** But the name lands. Something cracks for one moment. | Maya does not step forward. She says the name quietly. She lets the silence after it do the work. Her eyes are steady. | Sara pauses mid-turn. Her shoulders drop half an inch. She almost looks back. Then she straightens, nods without turning around, and walks toward the parking lot. Her pace is slightly too fast — she is outrunning the feeling. |

**Subtext throughout:**

- Maya: "I forgave you. I need you to let me."
- Sara: "If I face what I did, it becomes real. I am not ready."

**Direction note for Maya:**

```text
You want to reach her, but your obstacle is that pushing too hard will drive her away. Try to invite — not demand. Stay open and still. Let your vulnerability do the work. Do not chase when she walks away. The restraint is the strength.
```

**Direction note for Sara:**

```text
You want to escape without feeling anything. Your obstacle is that Maya is being kind, and kindness is harder to resist than anger. Try to deflect by keeping everything short and surface-level. When she says your name, you almost break — your body wants to turn around. Let the audience see the impulse and the suppression. The walk away should feel like it costs you something.
```
