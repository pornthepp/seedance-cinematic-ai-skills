# Representation Review Guide

Use this for full character, culture, cast, or fictional-world audits.

## Core Checks

### Agency

- Does the character want something beyond serving another character?
- Can they make meaningful choices?
- Do they change events?
- Are they allowed intelligence, humor, flaws, fear, skill, and contradiction?

### Specificity

- Are cultural references researched, fictionalized with care, or vague decoration?
- Does clothing fit climate, labor, status, history, and personal taste?
- Are names, symbols, scars, hair, makeup, props, and gestures meaningful?
- Does the character have ordinary life as well as dramatic function?

### Social Frame

- Who is treated as beautiful, dangerous, comic, disposable, wise, childish, or monstrous?
- Are marginalized characters only villains, victims, helpers, or lessons?
- Are flaws individual rather than framed as proof of group inferiority?
- Does the camera or description treat the character with the same dignity given to others?

### Visual Treatment

- Does the silhouette communicate personality and status, not just stereotype?
- Does lighting or framing exaggerate threat, exoticism, helplessness, or sexuality without story purpose?
- Do costume, props, and color choices have cultural, practical, and emotional logic?
- Is body type, age, skin tone, disability, class, or accent used only as a shorthand?

## Common Failure Modes

- Identity as costume only.
- "Exotic" culture with no ordinary life.
- One representative asked to carry a whole group.
- Trauma as the only depth.
- Sexualization used as the main personality.
- Disability, age, body size, class, or accent used only as weakness or joke.

## Upgrade Moves

- Add work, family, craft, humor, routine, and private desire.
- Give the character a decision with cost.
- Add relationships that reveal different sides.
- Make costume and silhouette serve status, environment, and personality.
- Spread power, vulnerability, beauty, competence, and contradiction across the cast.

## Scoring Rubric

Use this rubric for structured audits. Score each area 1-5. A character or cast scoring below 3 in any area needs redesign.

### Per-Character Score

| Area | 1 (Harmful) | 2 (Weak) | 3 (Functional) | 4 (Strong) | 5 (Excellent) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Agency** | Exists only to serve, die for, or educate another character. No independent desire. | Has a stated desire but never acts on it. Choices do not affect the story. | Has a clear want and makes at least one meaningful choice. | Makes choices with consequences. Has contradictions — wants conflicting things. | Drives story events through their own decisions. Changes other characters. Has a private life the audience glimpses. |
| **Specificity** | Cultural identity is a single visual shorthand (costume, food, accent) with no depth. | Some cultural detail, but generic — could be "any person from [region]." | Cultural references are specific enough to identify a real place, class, and era. Clothing and behavior have logic. | Cultural details reveal character — personal taste within cultural context, individual variation, ordinary routines. | The character could only be this specific person from this specific background. Culture informs but does not define. |
| **Social Frame** | Character's marginalized identity IS their narrative function (the victim, the lesson, the exotic helper). | Identity is addressed but only through suffering or teaching moments. | Identity is present but does not dominate the character's story function. They have a job, a goal, relationships beyond their identity. | Identity creates specific story possibilities without reducing the character. Their position in the world generates unique perspective and conflict. | Identity and narrative function are inseparable in a way that serves the character, not the audience's education. The character is not "about" their identity — they live through it. |
| **Visual Design** | Relies entirely on stereotype visual coding. A silhouette or costume that communicates only "type." | Some individual design, but the first read is still a cultural or social type. | Design has practical logic (climate, profession, personal taste) and communicates personality beyond identity. | Design reveals contradiction, aspiration, or inner state. The character's clothes tell a story that dialogue never states. | Every visual element — silhouette, color, texture, wear — serves character, story, and world simultaneously. Removing any element would lose information. |
| **Narrative Role** | One-function role: mentor who dies, comic relief, love interest with no arc, villain motivated only by identity. | Has a secondary function beyond the primary type, but it is underdeveloped. | Serves a clear narrative function AND has at least one scene that is about them, not about the protagonist. | Has their own mini-arc. The audience could imagine a version of the story told from their perspective. | Integral to the story structure. Their removal would collapse the plot, not just reduce diversity. |

### Cast Pattern Score

| Area | 1 (Harmful) | 3 (Functional) | 5 (Excellent) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Death distribution** | The marginalized characters die first or most often. | Deaths are distributed without demographic pattern. | Death serves individual narrative arcs, not genre conventions about who is expendable. |
| **Complexity distribution** | One demographic group gets inner life; others are functions. | Most characters have at least one contradiction or private moment. | Complexity is distributed across the cast. No demographic group is flattened. |
| **Power distribution** | Authority, intelligence, and beauty cluster in one demographic; others serve, assist, or fail. | Power types are distributed across demographics without rigid patterns. | Different characters hold different kinds of power (institutional, emotional, physical, social, moral). No demographic monopoly. |
| **Screen time distribution** | Marginalized characters exist in supporting scenes only. They do not drive sequences. | Supporting characters have at least one scene of their own. | Screen time reflects narrative importance, not demographic hierarchy. |

## Genre-Specific Red Flags

Different genres have different failure patterns. Check these based on your project:

### Action / Thriller

- [ ] Are characters of color used primarily as physical threats (the enforcer, the gang member)?
- [ ] Does the female lead's competence only exist through proximity to the male lead?
- [ ] Are foreign cultures used only as backdrops for danger (the dangerous city, the exotic weapon)?
- [ ] Is the only Asian character a tech expert or martial artist?

### Horror

- [ ] Does the Black character die first?
- [ ] Is the sexually active character punished with death?
- [ ] Is disability or mental illness used as the source of horror?
- [ ] Are indigenous or non-Western spiritual practices used as the monster's origin without cultural context?

### Romance

- [ ] Is the only queer character defined entirely by their queerness?
- [ ] Is the person of color a stepping stone for the white character's growth?
- [ ] Is the larger-bodied character the comic friend with no love story?
- [ ] Is the older character used only as a wisdom dispenser?

### Sci-Fi / Fantasy

- [ ] Are non-human species coded as specific real-world ethnicities (warrior race = Black, intellectual race = Asian, primitive race = indigenous)?
- [ ] Is the only female character in the team the emotional one or the healer?
- [ ] Are darker-skinned characters associated with darker moral alignment?
- [ ] Is the advanced civilization always coded as European/Western?

### Drama / Social Realism

- [ ] Is poverty only depicted through the lens of a middle-class or wealthy protagonist's discovery?
- [ ] Is the working-class character's dignity limited to their suffering?
- [ ] Is the immigrant character's story only about immigration?
- [ ] Is the disabled character inspirational rather than complicated?

## Audit Workflow

When conducting a full audit, follow this sequence:

1. **List every named character** with their demographic attributes (as described or implied) and narrative function.
2. **Score each character** using the per-character rubric (Agency, Specificity, Social Frame, Visual Design, Narrative Role).
3. **Map the cast pattern** using the cast pattern score (Death, Complexity, Power, Screen Time distributions).
4. **Check genre-specific red flags** using the appropriate checklist above.
5. **Identify the biggest single risk** — the one change that would most improve representation across the project.
6. **Propose concrete upgrades** — specific changes to story, design, dialogue, or structure. Not "be more diverse" but "give Character X a decision in Scene 3 that costs her something."

## Anti-Pattern: The Diversity Checklist Trap

A cast that checks every demographic box but gives complexity to only one character is worse than a smaller, more specific cast. Representation is not solved by presence — it is solved by the quality of narrative investment in each character.

Audit questions to catch this trap:

- Could each character's scenes be played by any actor of any background without changing the story? If yes, their identity is decorative.
- Does any character exist primarily so the project can claim representation? If yes, that character needs genuine narrative integration or honest removal.
- Is there at least one character whose cultural background generates a story problem only they can solve — not through cultural stereotypes (hacking, martial arts, cooking) but through perspective, history, or social position?
