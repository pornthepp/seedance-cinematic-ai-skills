# Ethical Character Representation Examples

Worked examples showing input and full output.

## Example 1: Full Character Audit

### User Input

> I have a Thai grandmother character in my short film. She runs a noodle shop.
> She's wise, gives advice to the young protagonist, and dies at the end to
> motivate his journey. Review this character.

### Output

**Audit:**

| Area                 | Current Reading                                                                                                                                             | Risk Level | Issue                                                                                                                                                                                                                               | Upgrade                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              |
| -------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Agency               | She exists to serve the protagonist's arc. She gives wisdom, then dies so he can grow.                                                                      | **High**   | This is the "Magical Minority Elder" pattern — a marginalized character whose only function is to dispense wisdom and then be sacrificed for the protagonist's development. She has no desire of her own.                           | Give her a want that has nothing to do with the protagonist. She is trying to renegotiate her shop lease. She is learning to use a smartphone to video-call her sister in Chiang Rai. She is hiding a health problem because she does not want her family to worry. Any of these makes her a person with her own story.                              |
| Visual design        | Not specified, but the "Thai grandmother noodle shop" combination defaults to a set of visual clichés: apron, gray hair in a bun, wooden spoon, warm smile. | **Medium** | The visual defaults are not offensive, but they are generic. She looks like every other "Asian grandmother in a food establishment" character.                                                                                      | Specify details that make her individual. She wears reading glasses on a beaded chain that she made herself. She has a specific hairstyle — maybe short and dyed dark, because she is vain about her age. Her apron has a logo from a supplier, not a generic pattern. She keeps a small portable radio tuned to a specific station while she cooks. |
| Cultural specificity | "Thai grandmother" is the only cultural marker. The noodle shop is generic.                                                                                 | **Medium** | Thai food culture is extremely specific — boat noodles, kuay tiew, khao soi, pad thai all come from different regions and traditions. "Noodle shop" with no specificity reduces Thai culture to a backdrop.                         | What kind of noodles? Where is the shop — Bangkok street stall, Chiang Mai shophouse, or a market? What time does she start prep? Is it a family recipe? Regional specificity makes the culture real instead of decorative.                                                                                                                          |
| Narrative role       | Mentor/sacrifice. She teaches, then dies.                                                                                                                   | **High**   | Her death is for him, not for her. The audience is trained to feel sad about her death only because it affects the protagonist, not because she was a full person whose absence is a genuine loss.                                  | Option A: She does not die. Find another way to launch the protagonist's journey. Option B: If she dies, the death must cost HER something — she had unfinished business, an unresolved relationship, something she wanted to do. Her death should feel like the loss of a person, not the loss of a function.                                       |
| Social framing       | She is coded as warm, selfless, and available.                                                                                                              | **Medium** | The "endlessly nurturing Asian grandmother" is a pattern that denies older women complexity. Real grandmothers can be impatient, opinionated, manipulative, funny, sharp, petty, and generous — often all in the same conversation. | Let her be difficult. She gives advice that is wrong sometimes. She has an opinion about the protagonist's choices that comes from her own bias, not from wisdom. She nags. She is stubborn about something irrational. She is a person, not a function.                                                                                             |
| Cast/world pattern   | She appears to be the only Thai character described. The protagonist's identity is not mentioned.                                                           | **Medium** | If the protagonist is non-Thai and the Thai grandmother is his sole connection to Thai culture, she becomes an access point rather than a person — she exists to enrich his experience.                                             | Check: does the world have other Thai characters with their own concerns? Does the noodle shop have regulars who are Thai? Is the neighborhood real? One Thai character in a non-Thai world carries an unfair representative burden.                                                                                                                 |

**Summary:** The character has two structural problems — she has no agency
independent of the protagonist, and her death serves his arc instead of her own.
These are both fixable without changing the core story. The cultural specificity
issue is less severe but easy to improve.

**Strongest single upgrade:** Give her a secret. Something she wants that she
has not told anyone. It does not need to dominate the story — it just needs to
exist. When she dies, the audience loses not just "the wise grandmother" but a
person with unfinished private business. That is the difference between a symbol
and a character.

## Example 2: Cast Pattern Review

### User Input

> My sci-fi short has 5 characters. Can you check the overall cast for
> representation issues?
>
> - Captain Reyes (Latina, 40s, commander, makes all the decisions)
> - Dr. Kim (Korean, 30s, science officer, quiet, follows orders)
> - Okafor (Nigerian, 20s, engineer, comic relief, dies first)
> - Vasquez (Mexican, 50s, grizzled veteran, sacrifices himself)
> - Elena (Russian, 20s, pilot, love interest for Captain)

### Output

**Cast pattern analysis:**

| Character     | Power               | Humor        | Death             | Complexity                                                            | Romance                       | Problem                                                                                                                   |
| ------------- | ------------------- | ------------ | ----------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Captain Reyes | All decisions       | None         | Survives          | High (commander with weight of choices)                               | Active partner                | Carries too much narrative weight as the only complex character                                                           |
| Dr. Kim       | Follows orders      | None         | Survives          | Low — "quiet and follows orders" is not a character, it is an absence | None                          | Model minority pattern: Asian character is competent, silent, compliant, and underdeveloped                               |
| Okafor        | None                | Comic relief | Dies first        | Low — his function is comedy then death                               | None                          | Black character as comic relief who dies first is one of the most documented harmful patterns in sci-fi/horror            |
| Vasquez       | Military competence | None         | Sacrificial death | Medium — but defined entirely by toughness                            | None                          | "Grizzled Latino veteran who dies nobly" is a recognizable trope. His sacrifice serves the survivor's story, not his own. |
| Elena         | Vehicle control     | None         | Survives          | Low — defined by her relationship to the captain                      | Defined by love interest role | Woman whose primary narrative function is being the love interest                                                         |

**Pattern problems across the cast:**

1. **Who dies?** Both characters who die are men of color (Okafor and Vasquez).
   Both surviving men-of-color characters (Dr. Kim) have minimal presence. The
   people of color in this cast serve, sacrifice, or stay silent. This pattern
   is more damaging than any individual character choice.

2. **Who gets to be funny?** Only Okafor. Making the Black character the sole
   comic relief — especially when he also dies first — reduces him to a
   mood-lightening device. Humor should be distributed across the cast or given
   narrative weight.

3. **Who gets complexity?** Only Captain Reyes. Every other character is one
   function: science, comedy, toughness, romance. In a 5-character cast, at
   least 3 characters need internal contradiction to avoid feeling like a type
   lineup.

4. **Who gets romance?** Only the two women, and it defines Elena's role. No
   other character has a personal life, desire, or relationship outside their
   job function.

**Upgrade priorities:**

| Priority     | Action                                                                                                                                                                                                                           |
| ------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Critical** | Do not kill Okafor first. If someone must die first, it should not be the Black comic-relief character. Either change who dies, or change his role so he is not comic relief.                                                    |
| **Critical** | Give Dr. Kim agency. He should disagree with the captain, have a competing theory, make a decision that matters, or break a rule. "Quiet and follows orders" is not representation — it is erasure.                              |
| **High**     | Give Vasquez a reason to live. His sacrifice means more if the audience knows what he is losing. A daughter he talks about. A plan for after the mission. Something that makes his death a genuine cost, not a genre obligation. |
| **High**     | Give Elena a function that is not romance. She is a pilot — put her skill at the center of a crisis. Let her competence drive a scene. The romance can exist without being her primary narrative identity.                       |
| **Medium**   | Distribute humor. Let Reyes be funny. Let Kim have a dry joke. Humor is a sign of intelligence and humanity — concentrating it in one character and distributing seriousness to everyone else creates an imbalance.              |

## Example 3: Checking A Character Design Prompt For Bias

### User Input

> My image prompt: "A Middle Eastern merchant in flowing robes standing in a
> dusty marketplace, mysterious expression, dark eyes, ornate jewelry, exotic
> spices on display." Is this okay?

### Output

**Issue-by-issue breakdown:**

| Element                   | Problem                                                                                  | Why It Matters                                                                                                                                                                                                                    | Fix                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| ------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| "Middle Eastern merchant" | Collapses a region of 400+ million people across 17+ countries into one generic identity | A Lebanese jewelry designer, a Yemeni coffee trader, and a Turkish carpet maker have nothing in common except Western grouping. Specificity respects the culture.                                                                 | Choose a specific country, city, and trade. "An Omani frankincense trader in Nizwa souq" or "A Moroccan leather craftsman in the Fez medina."                                                                      |
| "Flowing robes"           | Defaults to the visual cliché of "Arabian Nights" costuming                              | Not everyone in the Middle East wears robes. Clothing varies by country, climate, urban/rural setting, generation, profession, and personal taste. A merchant in Beirut dresses differently from a merchant in Sana'a.            | Research the specific clothing for the chosen location and profession. A Moroccan leather worker might wear a djellaba with pushed-up sleeves and stained hands. An Omani trader might wear a dishdasha and kumma. |
| "Mysterious expression"   | Orientalist framing — the "inscrutable Eastern other"                                    | "Mysterious" applied to a Middle Eastern face carries a specific colonial-era coding: unknowable, possibly deceptive, romantically dangerous. A Western merchant would be described as "shrewd" or "confident," not "mysterious." | What is the character actually feeling? Focused on a customer? Tired at the end of a long day? Proud of a display? Bored? Replace "mysterious" with a specific, human emotional state.                             |
| "Dark eyes"               | Racial feature used as aesthetic descriptor                                              | Describing only the darkness of the eyes without other facial detail reduces the face to a racial marker.                                                                                                                         | Describe the full face: expression lines, facial hair style, skin weathering from sun, the way they look at the customer or the merchandise. Eyes are part of a face, not the whole identity.                      |
| "Ornate jewelry"          | Decorative Orientalism                                                                   | "Ornate" positions the culture as decorative excess. Jewelry in Middle Eastern cultures has specific meaning — tribal affiliation, marital status, regional craft tradition, personal wealth signaling, religious significance.   | What specific jewelry and why? A Yemeni jambiya (curved dagger) indicates tribal status. Omani silver is a distinct craft tradition. Be specific about what is worn and what it means.                             |
| "Exotic spices"           | "Exotic" is a framing word, not a description                                            | "Exotic" means "foreign to me." Spices are not exotic to the person selling them — they are their daily trade. The word positions the viewer as Western and the subject as other.                                                 | Name the actual spices: saffron threads in a glass jar, ground turmeric in a brass bowl, dried rosebuds in a woven basket. Specificity replaces exoticism with reality.                                            |

**Revised prompt:**

```text
A Moroccan spice merchant in his 50s standing behind his stall in the Marrakech medina. He wears a faded olive-green djellaba with sleeves pushed above his elbows, revealing weathered hands stained yellow at the fingertips from handling turmeric. His face is tanned and lined, with a short gray beard and reading glasses pushed up on his forehead. He is arranging small brass bowls of ground cumin, paprika, and ras el hanout on a wooden display shelf. His expression is focused and habitual — he has done this arrangement a thousand times. Behind him, burlap sacks of dried herbs lean against a tiled wall. Late afternoon sunlight enters from the left, catching the dust and spice particles in the air. Natural, warm, documentary feeling.
```

**What changed:** Every orientalist shortcut became a specific, researched,
human detail. The character is from somewhere real, wearing something specific,
doing something concrete, and feeling something ordinary. He is a person at
work, not a cultural decoration.
