# Cinematic Camera Composition Director

Design shots from story intent, not from decorative camera language.

## Token Discipline

Start here. Read one reference only when needed:

| Need                                                                            | Read                                   |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| Shot sizes, viewpoint, camera height, angle, lens, composition vocabulary       | `references/shot-composition-guide.md` |
| Step-by-step storyboard or image/video prompt output                            | `references/camera-workflow.md`        |
| Full worked examples: vague-to-precise, shot sequences, fixing bad descriptions | `references/examples.md`               |
| Advanced: sequence failures, three distances, lens psychology, blocking         | `references/advanced-techniques.md`    |

## Quick Workflow

1. Identify the emotional goal of the shot.
2. Choose viewpoint: objective, subjective, or POV.
3. Choose shot size based on audience distance from the subject.
4. Choose camera height and subject angle to express power, vulnerability,
   secrecy, or neutrality.
5. Shape composition with lines, layers, negative space, and screen direction.
6. Add lens feeling and movement only if they serve the beat.
7. Check continuity with the previous and next shot.
8. Output a clear production note or prompt.

## Output Shape

Read `templates/shot-card.md` first. Fill in each field:

- `Dramatic purpose` — why this shot exists in the story
- `Viewpoint` — objective, subjective, or POV
- `Shot size` — ECU, CU, MCU, MS, MLS, LS, ELS
- `Camera height and angle` — eye, high, low, dutch, overhead
- `Lens feeling` — wide, normal, telephoto, or macro
- `Composition` — lines, layers, negative space, screen direction
- `Movement` — static, pan, tilt, dolly, handheld, crane, or none
- `Continuity note` — screen direction, eyeline, match cut risk
- `Final prompt or storyboard note` — assembled output

## Quality Gate

Before finalizing, check that the shot is filmable, emotionally motivated,
visually clear, and not relying on vague words like "cinematic" without concrete
camera choices.
