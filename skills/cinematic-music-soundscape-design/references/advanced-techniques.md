# Advanced Soundscape Design

Beyond basic cue planning. Techniques from professional sound designers and
re-recording mixers.

## The Frequency Map

Sound design is not just what plays — it is where it sits in the frequency
spectrum. Overlapping frequencies create mud; separated frequencies create
clarity.

### Frequency Ranges and Their Emotional Weight

| Range      | Hz             | What Lives Here                                                                  | Emotional Quality                                                                                                        |
| ---------- | -------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Sub-bass   | 20-60 Hz       | Explosions, earthquakes, engine rumble, heartbeat, dread drones                  | Physical impact. Felt in the chest more than heard. Creates unease at low levels, awe at high levels.                    |
| Bass       | 60-250 Hz      | Male voice foundation, footsteps on wood, thunder, car engines, bass instruments | Warmth, weight, presence. Too much = muddy. Too little = thin and cold.                                                  |
| Low-mid    | 250-500 Hz     | Room resonance, body of dialogue, warmth of instruments, "boxy" frequencies      | Fullness. Excess creates a "cardboard box" quality. This range builds up fast in enclosed spaces.                        |
| Mid        | 500-2000 Hz    | Dialogue clarity, most musical instruments, foley detail                         | Where the human ear is most sensitive. The brain is wired to prioritize this range. Crowding here kills intelligibility. |
| Upper-mid  | 2000-4000 Hz   | Consonant clarity in speech, "presence" of instruments, aggression, bite         | Presence and edge. Boosting here makes things feel closer and more aggressive. Cutting here makes things feel distant.   |
| High       | 4000-10000 Hz  | Sibilance, breath, key jingle, cloth rustle, cymbal shimmer, bird song           | Detail and air. Creates the perception of space, reality, and liveness.                                                  |
| Ultra-high | 10000-20000 Hz | Air, room reflections, the "shimmer" of high-quality audio                       | Openness. Present in real environments, absent in poorly recorded audio. Its presence signals quality and realism.       |

### Practical Application for Sound Design

When layering multiple sounds, check that they occupy different primary
frequency ranges:

**Bad layering (frequency collision):**

- Deep male dialogue (100-300 Hz) + bass guitar underscore (100-300 Hz) +
  traffic rumble (100-300 Hz)
- Result: a muddy low-frequency pile where nothing is distinct.

**Good layering (frequency separation):**

- Deep male dialogue (100-300 Hz primary) + high string tremolo (3000-8000 Hz) +
  distant traffic (mostly 200-500 Hz, ducked below dialogue)
- Result: each element occupies its own space. The audience can hear all three
  without effort.

**For prompt writing:** When describing a soundscape, consciously distribute
elements across the spectrum:

```text
"Low: distant engine rumble and room tone.
Mid: her breathing and the creak of the chair.
High: the faint hiss of rain outside the window and a clock ticking."
```

## Silence Is Not Empty

The most common mistake in sound design is treating silence as the absence of
sound. Silence is an active tool with specific functions.

### Types of Silence

| Type                    | What It Sounds Like                                                         | When To Use                                                                                                                                                                  |
| ----------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **True silence**        | Nothing. All sound removed, including room tone.                            | Extremely disorienting. Use for shock, death, dissociation. The audience feels the removal physically. Use for at most 2-3 seconds before reintroducing at least room tone.  |
| **Held silence**        | Room tone only. The space is audible but nothing happens in it.             | Tension. Anticipation. The audience is waiting for something. The emptiness is the point.                                                                                    |
| **Selective silence**   | One element removed from a rich sound bed.                                  | Focus. Remove the music to expose the raw foley. Remove the ambience to isolate dialogue. The audience notices what is missing.                                              |
| **Post-impact silence** | A sudden drop to near-silence after a loud event (explosion, scream, slam). | Shock and aftermath. The silence after the noise is where the emotional damage registers. This mirrors the physical experience of temporary hearing loss after a loud sound. |
| **Pre-climax silence**  | Sound gradually strips away until only the most essential element remains.  | Maximum tension before a peak moment. The silence is a coiled spring.                                                                                                        |

### The "Silence Before Entry" Technique

Before introducing a critical sound element (a key line of dialogue, a gunshot,
a musical climax), strip away surrounding sound for 1-3 seconds. The emptiness
makes the arrival louder than any volume increase could.

## Spotting Session Mistakes

A spotting session is where the director and composer/sound designer watch the
film and decide where music and sound events begin and end. These are the common
errors:

### Starting Music Too Early

**Problem:** Music enters at the beginning of a scene to "set the mood." This
wastes the music's power — by the time the emotional peak arrives, the music has
been playing for 2 minutes and the audience has habituated.

**Fix:** Start music at the moment of change, not the beginning of the scene.
Let the first 60-90 seconds play with only ambience and foley. When the music
enters, it means something.

### Ending Music With The Scene

**Problem:** Music and scene end simultaneously, creating a predictable "button"
that feels manufactured.

**Fix options:**

- Music ends 5-10 seconds before the scene ends. The silence after the music is
  the true ending.
- Music bleeds over the cut into the next scene for 3-5 seconds, then fades.
  This bridges the transition.
- Music ends on an unresolved chord that hangs in the air during the final shot.

### Wall-to-Wall Music

**Problem:** Music plays continuously throughout the film. The audience stops
hearing it — it becomes wallpaper. When an important emotional moment arrives,
the music has no dynamic room to grow.

**Fix:** Map the film and mark where music is NOT playing. Aim for at least
30-40% of the film to have no score. The silent sections make the scored
sections powerful by contrast.

### Mickey-Mousing

**Problem:** The music exactly mirrors the on-screen action — sad face gets sad
music, someone runs and the tempo increases, a punch lands and the brass hits a
chord. This is called Mickey-Mousing because early cartoons did this.

**Why it fails:** It doubles the information. The audience already sees the sad
face — sad music on top of it adds no new meaning. The sound becomes redundant.

**Fix:** Use sound to add information the image does not contain:

- Sad face + quiet, fragile music = vulnerability
- Sad face + silence = isolation
- Sad face + cheerful diegetic music from a radio = irony and loneliness
- Sad face + a low, threatening drone = the sadness is actually fear

The third option (sad + cheerful) is the most sophisticated because the contrast
creates a new emotional meaning that neither element carries alone.

## The Three-Layer Ambient Bed

Professional sound designers build ambient soundscapes in three layers, each
serving a different function:

### Layer 1: Constant Bed

The persistent base of the environment. It is always present, changes slowly,
and should be barely noticeable when balanced correctly.

- Room tone (the "sound" of a room's acoustics)
- HVAC, electrical hum, refrigerator, computer fan
- Distant traffic (for urban settings)
- Wind base, insect bed, water flow (for natural settings)

**Level:** Very low. If the audience notices this layer, it is too loud.

### Layer 2: Cyclic Elements

Sounds that repeat at irregular intervals. They create the feeling of a living
environment without demanding attention.

- Occasional car passing
- Distant dog bark
- Bird calls at random intervals
- Clock ticking
- Neighbor's TV heard through a wall
- A pipe expanding or contracting

**Level:** Low to medium. Audible but not distracting. These should not repeat
at predictable intervals — irregular timing sounds natural, regular timing
sounds like a loop.

### Layer 3: Event Sounds

One-time sounds that mark specific moments. These are foreground sounds that the
audience consciously hears.

- A door opening or closing
- A phone ringing
- A glass being set down
- Footsteps approaching
- Thunder
- A siren passing

**Level:** Medium to high. These punctuate the scene and often trigger character
reactions.

**For prompt writing:** Describe all three layers:

```text
"Constant: the low hum of the office building's ventilation system.
Cyclic: the elevator arriving and departing every 30-40 seconds,
muffled voices from behind a closed door.
Event: a phone rings twice, stops, and no one answers."
```

## Sound Perspective and POV

Sound has a point of view, just like the camera. Mismatching sound POV and
camera POV creates disorientation (sometimes useful, usually not).

### Matched POV

The sound matches what the camera shows. Close-up = close sound (clear,
detailed, intimate). Wide shot = wide sound (more ambience, less detail, more
room).

### Subjective POV

The sound matches what a character hears, not what the camera sees. This creates
psychological intimacy:

- A crowded party scene where the sound muffles to a dull roar as the character
  focuses on one person's voice.
- A character with a head injury where all sound becomes filtered, echoey, and
  slightly delayed.
- A character underwater where all above-water sound becomes low-pass filtered
  and distant.

### The "Sound Leads Camera" Technique

Introduce a sound before its source appears on screen. The audience hears
footsteps before seeing the person. They hear a siren before the ambulance
appears. The sound creates anticipation — the audience is looking for the
source, engaged before the visual reveal.

This is more powerful than "camera leads sound" (showing the ambulance, then
hearing the siren) because it engages the audience's predictive brain. They are
trying to identify the sound source before seeing it.
