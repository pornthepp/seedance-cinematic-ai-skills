# Music Soundscape Design Examples

Worked examples showing input and full output.

## Example 1: Sound Design For A Tension Scene

### User Input

> A character enters an empty house where something bad happened. I need the
> sound design. No music — just ambience and foley.

### Output

**Emotional sound intention:** Growing dread through absence. The house should
sound too quiet, then reveal traces of what happened through small, wrong
sounds.

**Lead audio element:** Silence and room tone, punctuated by foley.

**Sound timeline:**

| Timecode  | Layer    | Element                                                                                                                                                               | Level                 | Purpose                                                                                                     |
| --------- | -------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 0:00–0:05 | Ambience | Exterior street sounds — distant traffic, a bird — audible before the door opens                                                                                      | Medium                | Establish the normal world outside                                                                          |
| 0:05      | Foley    | Door handle turning, the click of the latch, the door swinging open on a slightly stiff hinge                                                                         | Medium-high           | The threshold moment. This sound should be precise and isolated.                                            |
| 0:06–0:08 | Ambience | Exterior sounds abruptly drop to near-silence as the door opens. Interior room tone takes over — a low, flat, dead space. No echo. Carpeted floor absorbs everything. | Low                   | The house swallows the outside world. The silence has weight.                                               |
| 0:08–0:15 | Foley    | Footsteps on carpet — soft, muffled, each one distinct. The character walks slowly. Two or three steps, then a pause. Then two more.                                  | Low-medium            | The rhythm of hesitation. The pauses matter more than the steps.                                            |
| 0:12      | Foley    | A floorboard creaks under the carpet on the third step                                                                                                                | Low                   | First surprise. The character freezes. The creak decays into silence.                                       |
| 0:15–0:20 | Ambience | A refrigerator hum becomes audible — it was always there but the audience notices it now in the silence                                                               | Very low, constant    | The house is not abandoned. The power is on. Someone was here recently.                                     |
| 0:20      | Foley    | A soft, irregular ticking — a clock on the wall, slightly fast, not quite steady                                                                                      | Very low              | Time is wrong in this place. The clock is the first sign that something is off.                             |
| 0:22–0:28 | Foley    | The character enters the kitchen. Shoe sole transitions from carpet to tile — sharper, more exposed. Each step has a slight sticky quality — something on the floor.  | Medium                | The floor texture tells us what happened before we see it. The sticky sound is the first physical evidence. |
| 0:28      | Foley    | A single drop of water from a faucet hitting a metal sink                                                                                                             | Medium-high, isolated | In the silence, this drop sounds enormous. The character stops.                                             |
| 0:30      | Ambience | The refrigerator compressor clicks off. Total silence for 2 seconds.                                                                                                  | Silence               | The most unsettling moment. Everything stops.                                                               |

**Spatial placement:**

- Room tone: wide stereo, enveloping
- Footsteps: center, close
- Clock: slightly off-center right, creating spatial unease
- Refrigerator hum: low, diffuse, slightly left
- Water drop: center, sharp, close — as if the audience is standing at the sink

**Mix note:** Keep everything quiet. The loudest sound in this scene should be
the door handle at the beginning. Everything after is a descent into lower and
lower volume. The audience will lean in. That is the tension.

**What silence does here:** Music would tell the audience "be scared." Silence
forces them to listen, which is scarier. Every tiny sound becomes evidence. The
audience does the emotional work.

## Example 2: Score Direction For An Emotional Climax

### User Input

> The final scene: a father and son reconcile after years of not speaking.
> They're sitting on a porch. I want music but I don't want it to be
> manipulative. What should the score do?

### Output

**Emotional sound intention:** Earned tenderness. The music should arrive late,
stay quiet, and leave space for the audience to feel rather than being told to
feel.

**What the score should NOT do:**

- Start when the scene starts. The conversation deserves silence first.
- Use a swelling string section. That is the score telling the audience "cry
  now."
- Resolve to a major key the moment they reconcile. Real reconciliation is not a
  clean resolution.
- Be continuous. Gaps in the music reflect gaps in their ability to connect.

**What the score SHOULD do:**

**Phase 1 — Silence (first 60% of the scene):**

No music. Only porch ambience: distant evening insects, a occasional car passing
on a far road, the creak of a porch chair, ice shifting in a glass. The audience
sits in the discomfort with them.

**Phase 2 — Entry (after the emotional turn):**

The music enters AFTER the key line is spoken, not during it. Let the words land
in silence first.

Entry instrument: solo acoustic guitar or solo piano. One instrument, one voice.
Not an orchestra. The reconciliation is between two people, and the score should
feel that intimate.

The first notes are simple — a short melodic phrase, 4-5 notes, in a minor key
that leans toward major without arriving. The melody should feel like it is
trying to remember something. Play it once, then pause. Silence. Then play it
again with a small variation — a note held longer, a chord voiced slightly
warmer.

**Phase 3 — Presence (final 20% of the scene):**

A second instrument joins — a soft cello or a second guitar playing a low
sustained note underneath. Not harmony — just warmth. The two voices do not play
the same rhythm. They coexist imperfectly, like two people learning to be in the
same space again.

The melody does not resolve. The scene ends with the music still open — a
suspended chord or an unfinished phrase. The reconciliation is beginning, not
complete. The score should feel like a door opening, not a door closing.

**Cue exit:** The music does not crescendo at the end. It stays at the same
quiet level and simply stops when the scene cuts. Or it carries over the cut to
black for 3-4 seconds, then fades. The last thing the audience hears is one held
note decaying naturally.

**Mix note:**

- Music level: beneath the ambience, not above it. The insects and porch sounds
  should remain audible through the score.
- No reverb wash on the instruments. Keep them dry and close, like someone
  playing in the next room.
- If either character speaks during the music, the score drops to
  near-inaudible. The voice always wins.

## Example 3: Sound Palette For A Whole Short Film

### User Input

> My 8-minute short film follows a street food vendor in Bangkok through one day
> — early morning prep to late night cleanup. I need a sound identity for the
> whole film.

### Output

**Sonic identity:** The city as a living organism that breathes around one
person. The vendor's sounds are precise and physical (chopping, sizzling,
clinking); the city sounds are layered, shifting, and never the same twice. The
film should sound like you are standing at the cart.

**Sound palette:**

| Category                   | Elements                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          | Character                                                                                                              |
| -------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Vendor's world             | Knife on cutting board (rhythmic, practiced), wok on fire (roaring gas, oil crackling), metal spatula scraping, plastic bags rustling, ice in a styrofoam cooler, coins dropped in a metal tin, ladle in broth                                                                    | Close, intimate, ASMR-detailed. These sounds are within arm's reach. They have rhythm — the vendor's work is music.    |
| City layer 1 — Street      | Motorbikes (2-stroke engines, distinctive Bangkok sound), tuk-tuks, songthaew horn patterns, bus air brakes, footsteps on concrete, flip-flops                                                                                                                                    | Medium distance. Constantly present but not overwhelming. The mix of engine types creates a texture unique to Bangkok. |
| City layer 2 — Human       | Thai conversation fragments (not subtitled, just texture), laughter from a neighboring stall, a child shouting, a vendor calling out prices, temple bell in the distance, a phone playing a Thai pop song on speaker                                                              | Varies from close (customers) to far (temple bell). Human presence surrounds the cart.                                 |
| City layer 3 — Environment | Morning: roosters, temple bells, monks' bare feet on pavement. Midday: heat hum, electric fan oscillating, flies. Evening: generator hum, fluorescent tube buzz, rain on corrugated tin (if rainy season). Night: fewer engines, dogs barking far away, a TV from a window above. | This layer tells the time of day. It is the clock of the film.                                                         |

**Time-of-day arc:**

| Time                   | Sound Density                                | Key Sound                                                          | Emotional Reading                          |
| ---------------------- | -------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------ |
| 4:30 AM — Prep         | Sparse. Single sounds in quiet.              | Knife on board in near-silence. A motorcycle in the distance.      | Solitude, ritual, calm focus               |
| 7:00 AM — Opening      | Building. Layers arrive one by one.          | Gas flame igniting with a whomp. First customer's voice.           | The day engine starts                      |
| 12:00 PM — Peak        | Dense. Overlapping, chaotic, energetic.      | Wok roar, three conversations, motorbikes, spatula rhythm          | Full immersion — overwhelming but musical  |
| 6:00 PM — Evening rush | Dense but shifting. Rain arrives (optional). | Rain on tin roof above the cart. Sizzle competes with rain.        | Second wind, endurance                     |
| 10:00 PM — Closing     | Thinning. Sounds withdraw one by one.        | Water from a hose washing the cart surface. Last motorbike fading. | Exhaustion, satisfaction, solitude returns |
| 11:30 PM — Walk home   | Sparse again. Back to single sounds.         | Footsteps on wet pavement. Keys jingling. A distant dog.           | Full circle. The city breathes out.        |

**Motif:** The vendor's chopping rhythm. It appears in the morning prep (slow,
deliberate), peaks at lunch (fast, automatic), and returns at the end when she
chops ingredients for tomorrow's prep (slow again, but now it carries the weight
of the whole day). Same sound, different emotional meaning each time.

**Mix philosophy:** Never mix the vendor's sounds and the city sounds at equal
level. One always leads. In work moments, the vendor's foley is foreground and
the city recedes. In rest moments (waiting for customers, wiping sweat, drinking
water), the city comes forward and her sounds drop away. The alternation creates
breathing room.
